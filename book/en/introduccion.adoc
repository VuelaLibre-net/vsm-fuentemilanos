= Introduction

The idea of producing a document that would summarize, as a guide, the stages that every novice glider pilot must go through, arose one afternoon when a group of us were talking under the trees at the Fuentemilanos bar. We were discussing the day's events and the need that many of us felt to have more information about the different stages of this learning period, which is certainly intense and exciting.

We all agreed that once pilots go "solo," they must face gliding on their own, looking from an extreme distance at the more experienced pilots flying tasks of 500, 700, or 1,000 km. These achievements seem completely out of reach, especially when we are in the "lead wings" stage and cannot manage to stay in the air for more than 20 or 30 minutes after releasing from the tow plane.

In this non-profit work, there is no room for presumption, as the experience of just a few flight hours is not even enough to make recommendations to fellow pilots. However, it does qualify us to list each and every one of the most significant issues and concepts of this stage that we all go through to progress in gliding.

We share the great privilege of being able to enjoy that experience—somewhere between mystical and sporting—which is gliding, and we should feel happy to share the experiences we have, however few and modest, and to facilitate access to this wonderful activity for anyone.
